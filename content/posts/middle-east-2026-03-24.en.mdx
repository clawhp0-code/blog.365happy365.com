## "I Want to Go Home" — The Words of a Syrian Refugee

Karim, a 35-year-old Syrian working in a small garment factory in Istanbul, Turkey, said in early 2026 that for the first time in 13 years, he was seriously considering going back. He said: *"I know Assad is gone. But I don't even know if my house is still there."* His single sentence compresses the reality Syria faces right now.

## After Assad — A Dramatic Transition, Yet an Incomplete Liberation


![Can Syria's Dream of Reconstruction Become Reality? — Syria at a Crossroads After Assad](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Bombed_out_vehicles_Aleppo.jpg/500px-Bombed_out_vehicles_Aleppo.jpg)


In December 2024, anti-government armed forces led by HTS (Hayat Tahrir al-Sham) launched a blitz offensive that ended the Assad regime's 54-year rule, and Bashar al-Assad fled to Russia. The international community cheered, but the cheering was short-lived.

Syria's reality is a tangled web. The northeast is controlled by Kurdish autonomous forces SDF, the northwest is held by the Turkish-backed Syrian National Army, and parts of the south remain under the influence of former regime remnants and tribal militias. The new interim government in Damascus is struggling to piece together this complex puzzle.

**Reconstruction costs alone: at least $400 billion.** That is the estimate from the World Bank and UN. But US and EU sanctions on Syria (such as the Caesar Act) have not been fully lifted, and investors remain cautious.

---

## Refugee Return: Miracle or Trap?

Syrian refugees worldwide number approximately 6 million. Statistics suggest about 500,000 returned in 2025 alone, but experts warn we should look at quality rather than quantity.

Many returnees have faced destroyed homes and confiscated property. Urban redevelopment laws like Decree 66, left behind by the Assad regime, served as legal grounds for seizing hundreds of thousands of homes, and the new government has yet to clearly resolve this issue.

*"Going back" and "being able to go back" are two different stories.*

## Syria on the Geopolitical Chessboard


![Related Image](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flag_of_Syria_%282025-%29.svg/500px-Flag_of_Syria_%282025-%29.svg.png)


The Syrian reconstruction issue is not a purely humanitarian one. Russia seeks to protect its interests at the Tartus naval base on the Mediterranean, while Iran is trying not to lose the land supply route connecting it to Hezbollah in Lebanon. Israel has taken advantage of the chaos to preemptively strike Iranian military infrastructure in Syria and expand buffer zones.

Meanwhile, Saudi Arabia and the UAE are opening economic cooperation channels with the new Syrian government and eyeing an expansion of influence. Qatar was among the first to return diplomats to Damascus. Syria has once again become a stage where great power interests intersect.

## Films and Dramas About the Middle East — Syria Through the Screen

The documentary **"For Sama" (2019)** is structured as video letters from a young female journalist to her newborn daughter Sama during the Aleppo siege. The scenes of giving birth and raising a child in a hospital where shells fall convey the raw horrors of war. A nominee for the Academy Award for Best Documentary Feature, this work reminds us why today's reconstruction discussions are not just about rebuilding structures.

**"The Square" (2013)** covers the Egyptian revolution, but resonates with the Syrian reality through its theme of "chaos after dictatorship." It sharply questions what remains after revolutionary fervor cools down.

The Israeli film **"Lebanon" (2009)** captures the terror of war as seen from inside a tank, touching on the horror of urban warfare that was repeated throughout Syria's civil war. However, these works are records of specific moments and perspectives, and should be viewed with the understanding that they do not represent the entirety of Syria's complex civil war.

## Is Spring Coming?

It has been over a year since the Assad regime's symbols were erased from the Syrian flag. Music flows again in Damascus cafes, and young people post dreams of a new Syria on social media. But the question of millions like Karim still hangs in the air.

Reconstruction is about building trust before building walls. For Syria's spring to become a true spring, it requires the lifting of international sanctions, the construction of an inclusive political system, and above all, the assurance that ordinary people can return home.

*The day that assurance takes hold, Karim will pack his bags too.*
