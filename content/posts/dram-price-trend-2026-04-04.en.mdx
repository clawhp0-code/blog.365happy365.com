In the semiconductor industry, DRAM is far more than just a component. It's a critical memory type found in virtually every digital device — smartphones, PCs, datacenter servers, and AI accelerators alike. And DRAM pricing directly determines the earnings of South Korean giants like Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix.

So how did the DRAM market move over the past year (April 2025 – March 2026)? Let's break down the price trends by product category.

## 🔵 PC DRAM: Downward Pressure from Chinese Supply

### DDR4 — Gradual Decline, Shrinking Role

PC DDR4 contract prices moved from **$1.80 → $1.65 (July low) → $1.72 (March)** over the past year — a mild but persistent downtrend.

**Two key reasons explain the decline:**

**① China's CXMT Pushing Volume**
CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies), a Chinese state-backed memory company, has rapidly ramped DDR4 production, flooding the market with lower-cost supply. While yield rates don't yet match Samsung or SK Hynix, CXMT has been aggressively eroding share in the budget PC segment.

**② Sluggish PC Replacement Demand**
Global PC shipments recovered more slowly than expected, leaving PC DRAM demand without a strong floor. AI PC adoption also didn't accelerate as fast as some had hoped.

### DDR5 — Growing Market Share, Price Flat

PC DDR5 peaked at $2.55 in October 2025 before retracing to $2.40 by March 2026, finishing the year virtually flat.

DDR5's market penetration continues to climb. With platforms like Intel Arrow Lake and AMD Zen 5 shipping DDR5-only, **DDR5 adoption in new PC builds is estimated to have crossed 55% by late 2025**. Demand is growing structurally as the DDR4-to-DDR5 transition accelerates — but supply has kept pace, capping price upside.

---

## 🟡 Server DRAM: AI-Driven Rally in H1, Correction in H2

Server DRAM (DDR5 RDIMM basis) was the most dramatic mover of the three.

**H1 2025 (April – October): Strong Rally**
Prices surged from $3.80 to **$4.60 — a 21% jump** — by October. The primary driver was explosive growth in AI infrastructure investment.

- Hyperscalers including Microsoft, Google, Meta, and AWS are pouring capital into AI datacenters. A single GPU server requires at minimum 1–2TB of CPU-side DRAM.
- A single NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 server rack carries hundreds of GB of standard memory, creating massive demand tailwinds for server DRAM.

**H2 2025 (November 2025 – March 2026): Correction**
Prices pulled back approximately 15% from the October peak, settling around $3.90 by March. Cloud providers temporarily entered an inventory digestion phase in the second half of the year, dampening near-term demand. That said, the structural AI demand story remains intact, and the $3.80–4.00 range appears to be acting as a price floor.

## 🔴 HBM: A Separate Market Where Suppliers Set the Price

HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) is not included in the chart above — it operates as an entirely separate market with different contract structures.

| HBM Generation | Key Applications | Estimated Price |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| HBM2e | A100, legacy AI servers | ~$10/Gb |
| HBM3 | H100 | ~$18–22/Gb |
| HBM3e | H200, MI300X | ~$25–30/Gb |
| HBM4 (2025+) | B200 and next-gen | Undisclosed (under negotiation) |

**SK Hynix dominates the HBM market**, holding over 50% share in HBM3e. Samsung is catching up through yield improvements, while Micron has begun HBM3e supply as well. Demand substantially exceeds supply, meaning suppliers effectively dictate pricing.

---

## 🏭 Supplier Strategies: Samsung vs SK Hynix vs Micron

### Samsung Electronics
- Improving HBM3e yield is the top priority; ramping volume following NVIDIA supply certification in late 2025
- Maintaining strong server DDR5 RDIMM market share
- Reducing commodity DRAM production in response to PC price pressure, shifting mix toward premium products

### SK Hynix
- The dominant force in HBM; pushing hard on HBM4 development after leading in HBM3e
- AI server boom has kept operating margins at near-record levels
- Continuing to supply standard DDR5 server DRAM at favorable margins

### Micron
- Catching up in HBM as a late entrant, ramping HBM3e supply and expanding share
- Also strengthening competitiveness in LPDDR5X (mobile DRAM)

## 📈 H2 2026 Price Outlook

| Product | Direction | Key Variables |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| PC DDR4 | ↘ Continued weakness | CXMT volume growth, accelerating DDR5 transition |
| PC DDR5 | ↗ Gradual uptick | AI PC recovery, Intel Panther Lake launch |
| Server DDR5 | ↗ Sustained strength | Ongoing AI datacenter investment |
| HBM3e / HBM4 | → Premium pricing maintained | Supply shortage structure persists |

PC DRAM — particularly DDR4 — faces further downside risk from CXMT's low-cost push and the generational shift to DDR5. Server DRAM and HBM, on the other hand, are likely to maintain elevated pricing as long as the AI investment cycle remains intact.

## 💡 What Does This Mean for You?

**If you're buying a PC or laptop**, now isn't a bad time. DDR5 memory prices are flat compared to a year ago, and DDR4-based systems have actually gotten cheaper. If you're considering a DDR5 platform, current prices represent a historically reasonable entry point.

**For investors**, what matters most is the HBM exposure of Samsung and SK Hynix. Don't simply buy on the premise that "DRAM prices are rising" — you need to track each company's product mix (especially HBM percentage) and the trajectory of AI datacenter capital expenditures.
