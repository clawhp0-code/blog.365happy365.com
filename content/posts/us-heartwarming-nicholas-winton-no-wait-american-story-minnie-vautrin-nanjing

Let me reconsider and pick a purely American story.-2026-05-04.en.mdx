## The First Stones

In 1968, in a small town in Maryland, a ten-year-old Black boy named Daryl Davis was marching in a Boy Scout parade. He carried a flag and walked proudly alongside his fellow scouts. Then, without warning, bottles, rocks, and cans came flying from the crowd along the road.

Daryl didn't understand at first. He looked around and realized he was the only Black scout in the troop. The projectiles were meant for him — and only him.

When he got home and told his parents what happened, they sat him down gently and explained: there are people in this world who will hate you simply because of the color of your skin. Young Daryl couldn't comprehend it. A question lodged itself in his mind that day, one that would define the rest of his life:

*"How can you hate me when you don't even know me?"*

---

## Music Opens Doors

Daryl fell in love with music early. He learned piano and became fascinated by the roots of rock and roll and blues — genres born from the interplay of Black and white musical traditions. Chuck Berry, Jerry Lee Lewis, Little Richard — in their music, Daryl heard proof that racial barriers could dissolve.

He grew up to become a professional musician, performing in bars, clubs, and lounges across the country. It was in 1983, at a country lounge in Frederick, Maryland, that a pivotal encounter took place.

After a set, a white man approached Daryl at the bar. "You play really well," he said. "This is the first time I've ever heard a Black man play piano like Jerry Lee Lewis."

Daryl smiled and shared something the man didn't know: Jerry Lee Lewis's piano style was actually rooted in Black boogie-woogie and blues. The two men talked, laughed, and drank together for the rest of the evening. Then the man leaned in and confessed:

**"You know, this is the first time I've ever sat down and had a drink with a Black man. I'm a member of the Ku Klux Klan."**

Daryl was stunned — but he didn't leave. Instead, he grew more curious. Before the night was over, the two men exchanged phone numbers.

## Sitting in the Enemy's Living Room

Where most people would have walked away permanently, Daryl walked toward. He made a deliberate, almost unimaginable decision: he would seek out KKK leaders and sit down with them, face to face, and simply talk.

In the early 1990s, he contacted Roger Kelly, the Grand Dragon (state leader) of the Maryland KKK, requesting an interview under the pretense of writing a book about the Klan. Kelly agreed — suspiciously.

At their first meeting, Kelly placed a pistol on the table between them. Daryl's Black secretary trembled by the door. But Daryl sat calmly and began to converse.

He didn't argue. He didn't lecture. He didn't try to convert. **He listened.** He asked questions. He shared his own story. He played music.

One meeting became two. Two became ten. They visited each other's homes, shared meals, attended each other's events. Over time, Roger Kelly found himself confronted with an impossible contradiction: everything he had been taught about Black people — that they were inferior, dangerous, fundamentally different — was contradicted by the man sitting across from him, laughing at the same jokes, loving the same music.

One day, Roger Kelly folded up his KKK robe and hood and handed them to Daryl Davis.

"I don't need these anymore."

Kelly didn't just leave the Klan. He and Daryl became genuine friends. Kelly eventually asked Daryl to be the godfather of his daughter.

---

## 200 Robes and Counting

Over the next three decades, Daryl repeated this process again and again. He met with Imperial Wizards, Grand Dragons, rank-and-file Klansmen, neo-Nazis, and members of the Aryan Nation. Some meetings were cordial. Others were dangerous — he has been threatened, had guns pulled on him, and narrowly escaped violent confrontations.

But he never stopped.

The results speak for themselves. **More than 200 KKK members and white supremacists have left their organizations**, many of them handing Daryl their robes and hoods as they walked away from hate. His home contains a collection of these garments — not as trophies, but as evidence that people can change.

Among those who left were high-ranking Klan officials. One former Klansman served as best man at Daryl's wedding.

---

## The Criticism and the Conviction

Daryl's approach has not been without controversy. Some Black activists have questioned why the burden of educating racists should fall on Black people. Others have worried about his safety and argued that his one-on-one approach cannot address systemic racism.

Daryl has always responded thoughtfully:

> *"I don't tell anyone else they have to do what I do. This is my path. But I am certain of one thing — when you sit down and talk with someone, you will eventually find common ground. When you find common ground, the differences become less important. And when the differences become less important, hatred loses its foundation."*

He has also said:

> *"If you are not prepared to sit down and talk with your enemy, you don't want change. You just want to win."*

His story was documented in the 2016 documentary *Accidental Courtesy: Daryl Davis, Race & America*, which brought his decades-long mission to an international audience. His TEDx talks have garnered millions of views.

## The Answer to a Ten-Year-Old's Question

As of 2024, Daryl Davis is in his seventies and still performing, still speaking, still meeting with people on the farthest edges of hate. In one room of his home, dozens of white KKK robes hang in a closet. They are not souvenirs. They are proof — proof that human beings, even those consumed by hatred, carry within them the capacity for transformation.

The question that a ten-year-old boy asked after being pelted with rocks at a parade — *"How can you hate me when you don't even know me?"* — became a life's mission. And over the course of fifty years, Daryl Davis found his answer:

**"They hated me because they didn't know me. So I decided to let them know me."**

The opposite of hate is not love. The opposite of hate is understanding. And understanding always — always — begins with a conversation.
