# Can Love Be Interpreted?

## Why I Chose This Title

This title was inspired by Sofia Coppola's film *Lost in Translation*. Like the unspoken emotions shared between the characters in that film, I began to think that love, too, sometimes cannot be perfectly translated into words.

Living between Korean and English, I've realized that the same emotions are expressed and received differently depending on the culture. The three syllables of "saranghae" and the three words "I love you" both carry the same feeling, yet their weight and meaning arrive slightly differently.

## Emotions That Can't Be Translated

![Image symbolizing relationships and connection](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1516595565805-46d9957cee69?w=800&h=500&fit=crop)

Love is the most universal yet most personal emotion in the world. But can it truly be fully carried over into another language, another culture?

**Love in Korean Culture:**
- Care and devotion expressed through silence
- Emotions conveyed through glances and gestures
- Saying "stop" while hoping they'll continue
- The unity of family-centered responsibility and love

**Love in Western Culture:**
- Clear expression and direct confessions of feeling
- Love that respects individual freedom and independence
- When someone says "No," they truly mean no
- Priority given to personal choice and romance

When these two meet, sometimes there's conflict, and sometimes beautiful harmony emerges.

## The "Lost in Translation" Phenomenon

![The boundary of language and culture](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1546182990-dffeafbe841d?w=800&h=500&fit=crop)

In the film *Lost in Translation*, the protagonists find themselves unable to communicate through language in the unfamiliar environment of Tokyo, Japan. Yet through silence, they reach a deeper understanding.

Love can be the same way:

- The preciousness of simply being beside someone without words
- Being misunderstood because of cultural differences, yet continuing to try
- Accepting that perfect understanding is impossible, and embracing that imperfection

When we realize that perfect translation is impossible, we can paradoxically find a deeper connection.

## Why I Chose a Korean Title

This blog is a space for capturing Korean culture and everyday life. That's why I wanted to pose this question in Korean. Rather than directly translating "Lost in Translation," I felt that asking "Can love be interpreted?" conveys a deeper, more delicate feeling.

The word "interpretation" (통역):
- More human and intuitive than simple "translation"
- Implies the role of someone mediating between two worlds
- Carries the will to keep trying, even if perfection is impossible

## The Essence of Love

Ultimately, the answer to this question is:

**Love cannot be interpreted. But that doesn't make it any less precious.**

Rather, a connection that transcends language, understanding that crosses cultural differences, and the courage to accept imperfection — isn't that true love?

Like the final scene of the film, sometimes **who you shared silence with** matters more than what was said.

---

**Final thought:**
Have you ever had a "Lost in Translation" moment in your life? Someone you couldn't explain in words but could feel in your heart?

If so, that is love that cannot be translated.
