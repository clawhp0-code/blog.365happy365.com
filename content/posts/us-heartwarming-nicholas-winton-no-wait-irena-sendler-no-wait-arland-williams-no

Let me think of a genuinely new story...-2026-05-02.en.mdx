# "Nine for Nine" — The Miracle Rescue of the Quecreek Miners, 2002

## The Water Came Without Warning

On the evening of Wednesday, July 24, 2002, eighteen miners were working in two crews deep inside the Quecreek Mine in Somerset County, Pennsylvania. At approximately 9:00 p.m., one crew accidentally breached the wall of the adjacent, long-abandoned Saxman Mine — sealed since 1957 and filled with tens of millions of gallons of groundwater.

The water exploded through the breach at a terrifying rate. Within minutes, the mine's lower passages were flooding rapidly.

Nine miners who were working closer to the exit managed to escape. But nine others — Blaine Mayhugh, Randy Fogle, Thomas Foy, John Unger, Ron Hileman, Robert Pugh, Mark Popernack, Dennis Hall, and Harry "Moe" Mayhugh — were driven deeper into the mine by the rising water. They retreated to the highest point they could find: a pocket of space roughly four feet high, 240 feet below the surface.

## Bound Together in Darkness

The water rose to their waists. The temperature hovered around 55°F (12°C). One by one, their headlamps flickered and died, plunging them into total darkness.

In that darkness, the nine men made a pact.

**"We all go home, or nobody goes home."**

They took their work ropes and belts and tied themselves to one another. If one man lost consciousness from hypothermia and slipped beneath the water, the others would feel the tug and pull him back. If they were all going to die, their families would find their bodies together.

Each man also wrote a farewell letter to his loved ones, sealed in whatever waterproof container he could find. Blaine Mayhugh later recalled: *"We decided we were going to live or die as a group. If it was the end, we wanted our families to find us together."*

## The Race to Drill Through 240 Feet of Rock

On the surface, a massive rescue operation mobilized within hours. Drilling experts, engineers, and volunteers converged on the small Somerset County community from across the country.

The plan was twofold: first, drill a six-inch borehole to pump compressed air into the trapped chamber and keep the men breathing; second, drill a much larger 30-inch rescue shaft through which each man could be lifted to safety in a specially built capsule.

The challenges were enormous. The first drill bit broke at 150 feet. The second broke as well. Specialty bits were flown in from across the United States. The large rescue drill wandered off course and had to be redirected. Every broken bit, every mechanical failure, cost precious hours — hours the men underground might not have.

Early on July 25, the six-inch air supply hole finally broke through into the mine chamber. Compressed warm air flowed down to the trapped miners for the first time. The men felt it, gathered around the pipe, and hammered on it.

**Nine taps. Nine men alive.**

On the surface, rescuers wept with relief — and redoubled their efforts on the larger shaft.

## 77 Hours

For more than three days, the drilling continued around the clock. Crews rotated in shifts, working through rain, mechanical failures, and exhaustion. The nation watched, transfixed. News networks broadcast live from the muddy field above the mine.

At approximately 1:00 a.m. on Sunday, July 28, 2002 — 77 hours after the mine flooded — the 30-inch rescue shaft finally broke through to the chamber below.

A specially fabricated yellow rescue capsule, just 22 inches in diameter, was lowered into the earth. One by one, each miner climbed into the capsule and was winched to the surface.

By 2:45 a.m., all nine men stood on the surface.

**"Nine for nine!"** The cry rang out across the rescue site, echoed by hundreds of rescuers, family members, journalists, and volunteers who had kept vigil through the long weekend.

Not a single life was lost.

## The Letters They Never Had to Send

When Blaine Mayhugh emerged from the capsule and embraced his wife Leslie, his first words were: *"Did you get my letter?"* She didn't understand. She later learned that he had written her a goodbye letter in the dark, believing he would never see her again.

None of those letters had to serve as final words. But the fact that they were written — carefully, in the dark, in freezing water, by men who didn't know if they had hours or minutes left — speaks to something profound about what matters to people at the very end. Not anger, not regret. Love.

## Aftermath and Legacy

In August 2002, President George W. Bush invited the nine miners and their rescuers to the White House, honoring their courage and the extraordinary solidarity shown by everyone involved in the rescue.

The miners co-authored a book titled *Our Story*, recounting their experience underground and the bonds that sustained them. They remained close friends for life, bound not only by rope but by a shared covenant forged in the darkest place any of them had ever been.

The yellow rescue capsule was donated to the Smithsonian National Museum of American History, where it remains on display — a testament to human ingenuity and the refusal to give up.

A memorial was erected at the Quecreek Mine site in Somerset County, Pennsylvania, bearing the names of the nine miners and all those who worked to bring them home.

## What the Rope Means

The Quecreek rescue is often called a miracle, and perhaps it was. But it was a miracle built on very human choices: the choice of nine men to tie themselves together rather than face death alone; the choice of hundreds of rescuers to keep drilling through broken bits and impossible odds; the choice of a community to stay awake, to keep praying, to refuse despair.

When the darkness comes — and it comes for everyone — the Quecreek miners offer a simple, powerful answer:

**Tie yourself to the person next to you. Live together or die together. But never let go.**

They never let go. And all nine came home.
