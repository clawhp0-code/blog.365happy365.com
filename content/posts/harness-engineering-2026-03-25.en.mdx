How many meters of wire are inside a modern car? Surprisingly, about **1,500 to 4,000 meters** -- up to 4 km of wire is densely packed inside. The technology of systematically bundling, routing, and protecting these thousands of wires is called **Harness Engineering**.

---

## What Is a Harness?

The word "harness" originally refers to the tack used on a horse. Just as leather straps are woven around a horse's body for control, bundling wires together and securing them along designated paths is called a **Wire Harness** or **Cable Harness**.

It's not just about bundling wires. A harness includes the following components:

- **Wires**: Signal lines, power lines, ground lines, etc.
- **Connectors**: Plugs and sockets that connect to each electronic component
- **Protective materials**: Tape, corrugated tubes, heat-shrink tubing, etc.
- **Mounting clips & brackets**: Parts that secure the harness to the vehicle body

The entire process of designing and manufacturing all these elements into a finished assembly is harness engineering.

---

## Why Is It Important?

![Automotive wire harness manufacturing process](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Wiring_harness_manufacturing.jpg/800px-Wiring_harness_manufacturing.jpg)
*Wire harnesses are mostly assembled by hand. (Source: Wikimedia Commons)*

What would happen without a harness? Thousands of wires would sprawl haphazardly throughout the vehicle, turning the engine bay into a jungle of wires. Vibration-induced wear, electrical leakage, and short circuits would occur, potentially leading to fires.

A well-designed harness ensures the following:

| Function | Description |
|---|---|
| **Safety** | Prevents leakage and short circuits, reduces fire risk |
| **Reliability** | Stable signal transmission in vibration, heat, and moisture environments |
| **Assembly Efficiency** | Installed as a single module in the factory |
| **Maintainability** | Quickly locate and replace faulty sections |
| **Weight Reduction** | Eliminates unnecessary wire length through optimized routing |

As the transition to electric vehicles (EVs) accelerates, the importance of harnesses grows further, because EVs have far more complex electrical and electronic systems than internal combustion engine vehicles.

## Key Tasks in Harness Engineering

### 1. Schematic Design
Circuit diagrams are created to map which wires connect to which components. Wire gauge (AWG or mm2) and color are specified based on voltage, current, and signal type.

### 2. 3D Harness Routing
The harness path is designed on a 3D model of the vehicle. The key challenge is finding the shortest route while avoiding engine heat sources, sharp edges, and moving parts (such as door hinges). Specialized software like **CATIA**, **NX**, and **Capital Harness** is used.

### 3. Terminal & Connector Selection
From hundreds of connector types, the right one is selected based on the circuit's current capacity, waterproofing rating (IP code), and operating temperature range.

### 4. Wrapping Material Design
Decisions are made on which protective materials to use for each section. For example, fiberglass sleeves are used in high-temperature engine bay sections, and grommets are used where the harness passes through the vehicle body.

### 5. Testing & Validation
Completed harnesses must pass the following tests:
- **Continuity test**: Verifies all circuits are properly connected
- **Insulation resistance test**: Checks for leakage between wires
- **Vibration test**: Simulates vehicle vibration environment
- **High/low temperature cycle test**: Confirms durability in extreme temperatures

## Where Is It Used?

Harness engineering is not limited to automobiles.

- **Aerospace**: A single Boeing 787 contains approximately **274 km** of wire
- **Defense**: Fighter jets, warships, missile systems
- **Industrial machinery**: Factory automation equipment, robots
- **Medical devices**: MRI machines, surgical robots, and other precision equipment
- **Home appliances**: Internal wiring of washing machines, air conditioners, etc.

---

## How to Become a Harness Engineer

Harness engineering is primarily entered by graduates in **Electrical/Electronic Engineering**, **Mechanical Engineering**, and **Automotive Engineering**. In South Korea, major harness specialist companies include Hyundai Mobis, Yura Corporation, LS Cable & System, and Kyungshin.

Key competencies required:

- Understanding of basic electrical circuits
- Proficiency in 3D CAD software (especially Capital series, CATIA)
- Understanding of vehicle architecture
- Knowledge of international harness standards such as IPC/WHMA-A-620

## Conclusion

A harness is the "nervous system" and "blood vessels" of a vehicle. It may be invisible and unglamorous, but without it, neither advanced electronic equipment nor autonomous driving systems can function at all. As the EV era advances, the weight, cost, and complexity of harnesses are emerging as key variables in vehicle competitiveness.

> **The technology that connects everything from behind the scenes -- that is harness engineering.**
