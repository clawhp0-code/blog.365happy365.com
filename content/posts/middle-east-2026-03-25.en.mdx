## Spring Has Not Yet Come to Damascus

In March 2026, metal barricades still stand in Damascus's Umayyad Square. Merchants in the old spice market Souq al-Hamidiyeh have cautiously begun opening their shops, but their eyes are a mixture of expectation and anxiety. Just over a year since the Assad regime fell, Syria is in the process of learning the word "freedom." But the people of this land know better than anyone that freedom does not automatically mean peace.

## The Ruins Left by 13 Years of Civil War
![Light and Shadow of Syria's Reconstruction: Where Is Damascus Heading After Assad?](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%AC%D8%A7%D9%85%D8%B9_%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%A3%D9%85%D9%88%D9%8A_%D9%81%D9%8A_%D8%AF%D9%85%D8%B4%D9%82.jpg/500px-%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%AC%D8%A7%D9%85%D8%B9_%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%A3%D9%85%D9%88%D9%8A_%D9%81%D9%8A_%D8%AF%D9%85%D8%B4%D9%82.jpg)


The Syrian civil war, which began during the "Arab Spring" of 2011, is recorded as one of the worst humanitarian catastrophes of the 21st century. Over 500,000 killed, more than 12 million refugees or internally displaced. The city centers of Aleppo, Homs, and Raqqa were devastated as badly as WWII-era German ruins. GDP evaporated by more than 60% compared to pre-war levels, and the power grid and water infrastructure effectively ceased to function.

In late 2024, a rebel coalition centered on HTS (Hayat Tahrir al-Sham) abruptly seized the capital, and Bashar al-Assad fled to Russia. The sight of the over-half-century Assad dynasty crumbling in just 11 days stunned the entire world.

## An Opportunity for Reconstruction, or More Chaos?

Ahmad al-Sharaa (formerly Abu Mohammed al-Julani), who leads the new transitional government, has been appealing to the international community for "inclusive governance" and "minority protection" while requesting the lifting of sanctions. Indeed, the US and EU began partially easing some humanitarian sanctions in the second half of 2025, and the Arab League also formalized Syria's readmission.

However, optimism is not the only voice. Territorial negotiations between the northeastern Kurdish autonomous forces SDF and the new government are deadlocked. Israel has pushed deeper into the Golan Heights buffer zone, and Turkey continues military operations under the pretext of Kurdish forces. Reconstruction costs are estimated at a minimum of $400 billion, but actual international investment remains minimal.

## The Stories of Ordinary People
![Related Image](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Chosin.jpg/500px-Chosin.jpg)


Khaled (42), an engineer from Aleppo, returned to his hometown after five years. Where his house once stood, only weeds grow. "Whatever happens with politics, I just want my children to go to school," he says. The essence of Syria's reconstruction is ultimately millions of stories like his. It is not about power dynamics — it is about whether people can return home.

International aid organizations are sensing subtle changes on the ground. Korean NGOs are conducting water purification and medical support projects in the region, reporting that Syrians' yearning for "normal life" has never been stronger.

## Films and Dramas About the Middle East

The work that most vividly records Syria's tragedy is undoubtedly the documentary **"For Sama" (2019)**. Filmed in the form of letters from a young female activist to her daughter "Sama" during the Aleppo siege, it simultaneously captured the horror of war and human dignity. The Academy Award-winning work resonated worldwide. The hopes of reconstruction and return being discussed in Damascus today begin right where this film left off.

**"The Cave" (2019)** is also an Aleppo-based documentary set in an underground hospital, following the dedication of Dr. Amani Ballour. These works carry even greater weight for being fiction-free records of reality as it was.

**"Lebanon" (2009)** explores the terror of war through the perspective of soldiers inside an Israeli tank, but in posing the universal question of "humanity within war," it connects to Syria's present. However, these works represent specific moments and viewpoints, and should be watched with the understanding that they do not speak for the entirety of Syria's complex civil war.

## Time Flows Differently in Damascus

Damascus, one of the oldest capitals in the world, has endured thousands of years of invasion and reconstruction. It survived the glory of the Umayyad Caliphate, the sieges of the Crusaders, and Ottoman rule. The reconstruction challenge this city now faces is unprecedentedly complex, but Syrians' will to return is also unprecedentedly strong. Spring has not yet arrived. But the seeds are already in the ground.
